## The teaching in one line

Self-sabotage is the most common and least-recognized reason spiritual seekers stall — it is the limited self's fear of the light, expressed as resistance, distraction, or inertia at the exact moments when transformation is available. Sourced primarily from Ra's 2024-01-23 YouTube live teaching "Self Sabotage & Spiritual Awakening" (1h 13m).

## The core framing: fear of the light

> *"The fear of the light is sometimes much more powerful than the fear of the dark. And there is that little part of us which becomes afraid of all the changes, the transformations, and tries to hold yourself back even against your best interest."*

Self-sabotage is not a character flaw. It is "that little part" — the limited self doing its job of keeping things manageable — operating against the seeker's consciously stated desire.

## Autobiographical anchor

> *"I was like, I am all in. I don't want anything else but God, but enlightenment. But another part of me was hiding in the shadows and conspiring to sabotage every time I wanted to do something about it. And in the beginning I didn't notice."*

Ra names the diagnostic blindspot: you cannot see your own self-sabotage from inside it. This becomes his central argument for mentorship.

## How seekers project self-sabotage

Common forms of the barrier, all of which Ra reframes as self-sabotage wearing philosophical clothing:

- *"I'm not worthy"*
- *"It's impossible / only for very rare people"*
- *"It's not possible in this age of Kali Yuga"*
- *"Only if you follow [specific prophet / specific book]"*

> *"Just because we believe in that barrier, it seems to be true. Just because we give our power to the self-sabotage, it has power."*

The barrier is not independent — it is animated by the belief in it.

## The scuba / free-diving metaphor

> *"The depth is sometimes really scary. When I learned free diving, it was so scary even to go below 20 feet. There was a physical reaction to come back up. But the more you stay calm, the more you stay centered... something starts finding a new comfort level. Something starts seeing the beauty of it."*

The surface = the known self; the depth = the true nature; the pull to return to the surface = the self-sabotage mechanism; growing comfort with depth = the work of practice and mentorship.

## The knowledge trap — subtle self-sabotage

> *"The need to know, the need to understand, can itself become self-sabotage. And it's one of the more subtle forms."*

Knowledge is welcomed — but the *need* to know before proceeding is the trap. Ra's own example: after a series of deep awakening experiences, he went on a quest to "reverse engineer everything about it," which became a way of delaying actual further practice through analysis.

## Two paths: warrior and devotee

| Approach | Orientation | Practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Warrior** | Control and awareness | Expand awareness to catch the sabotage pattern; become vigilant; act against the resistance |
| **Devotee** | Surrender | Give up the need to know; trust the process; release control |

> *"There's a time for the approach of the warrior and there's a time for the approach of the devotee. Both are important. Both are beautiful."*

Neither is universally correct — Ra reads the seeker and the moment.

## The mentor-as-mirror move

> *"Self-sabotage is hard to notice for oneself, at least in the beginning. But when somebody has gone through this journey and can see it from a third person's perspective, it's much more apparent to notice."*

The mentor's role is not to add content but to name what the seeker cannot see about their own pattern.

## Key phrases

- *"The fear of the light is sometimes much more powerful than the fear of the dark."*
- *"Just because we give our power to the self-sabotage, it has power."*
- *"Invite all of that — invite that anxiety, invite that fear, invite the doubt — but don't take it to be what it is on the surface. Take a step back, look at it, and see how it fits into this big picture."*
- *"The need to know, the need to understand, can itself become self-sabotage."*
